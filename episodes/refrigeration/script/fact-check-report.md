# Fact-Check Report: Refrigeration Episode

## Summary

**Claims checked:** 78 discrete factual claims across 5 chapter scripts
**Corrections made:** 6 (errors fixed in scripts)
**Unverifiable claims:** 4 (hedging added or recommended)
**Disputed claims:** 3 (documented below, scripts adjusted)
**Quotes verified:** 9 of 10 attributed quotes checked; 1 flagged as paraphrased with uncertain exact wording
**Overall assessment:** The scripts are factually strong. Most claims are well-sourced and verified. The primary issues are (1) a date range error on COVID vaccine waste, (2) an unverifiable specific seat-count claim for Sun Belt migration, (3) the Thermo King "$3 million business" figure refers to 1949 not 1961, (4) the Gorrie "crank" quote has variant wordings across sources, and (5) a specific typhoid death figure (635) could not be independently verified. No fabricated quotes were found. The Tudor diary quote, Thoreau passage, and Cold-Storage Banquet quotes are all verified from credible sources.

## Unverifiable Claims

### 1. Gorrie "Crank in Apalachicola" Quote -- Exact Wording
**Script (Ch03, line 44):** "There is a crank down in Apalachicola, Florida, that thinks he can make ice by his machine as good as God Almighty."
**Status:** The substance is verified -- a New York newspaper (most commonly attributed to the New York Globe) did attack Gorrie in these terms. However, the **exact wording varies** across sources: "crank down in Apalachicola" vs. "crank, a Dr. John Gorrie"; "as good as God Almighty" vs. "as good as the Lord Almighty"; with or without "by his machine." The Smithsonian's "Chilly Reception" article uses similar language. The quote is real in substance but may be slightly paraphrased from the original.
**Recommendation:** Keep as written. The script already attributes it to "A New York newspaper" rather than a specific publication, which is appropriately cautious. The substance is verified even if the exact wording is uncertain.

### 2. Berlin Family Killed by Refrigerant Fumes (Einstein Motivation)
**Script (Ch03, line 192):** "In Berlin, a family is killed by refrigerant fumes leaking from their home refrigerator."
**Status:** Multiple credible sources (APS, Smithsonian Lemelson Center, Wikipedia) confirm that Einstein was motivated by reports of a Berlin family killed by refrigerant fumes. However, no source provides the family's name, address, exact date, or which specific refrigerant caused the deaths. The general story is well-attested but lacks individual-level documentation.
**Recommendation:** Keep as written. The script uses appropriately general language ("a family"). No correction needed.

### 3. Tudor's Fortune -- "$200 Million in Today's Dollars"
**Script (Ch01, line 40):** "worth the equivalent of roughly two hundred million dollars in today's money"
**Status:** This figure appears in the New England Historical Society, Fox News, and several other sources. However, no source documents the inflation-adjustment methodology. The word "roughly" in the script provides appropriate hedging.
**Recommendation:** Keep as written. The hedging language "roughly" is appropriate for an undocumented inflation estimate.

### 4. "The one silver lining is that soon everyone who can remember what food is supposed to taste like, pre-refrigeration, will be dead."
**Script (Ch04, line 112):** Attributed to "one newspaper" after the Cold-Storage Banquet.
**Status:** This quote appears in the Atlas Obscura article sourced from Twilley's *Frostbite*. The specific newspaper is not named. The quote may be a paraphrase from Twilley's book rather than a direct newspaper quote.
**Recommendation:** Keep as written. The attribution to "one newspaper" is appropriately vague.

## Disputed Claims

### 1. Tudor's Debtor's Prison Visits: Twice or Three Times?
**Script (Ch01, line 32):** "at least twice"
**Status:** Wikipedia says Tudor spent time in debtor's prison "in parts of 1812 and 1813" (suggesting two stints). Investor Amnesia says "three times from 1809-1813." The discrepancy appears to depend on whether 1809 is counted as a distinct imprisonment.
**Resolution:** The script's use of "at least twice" is the most defensible phrasing. Verified as accurate. No change needed.

### 2. Texas Winter Storm Deaths: 246 vs. 700+
**Script (Ch05, line 42):** "At least two hundred and forty-six people were killed. Some estimates say over seven hundred."
**Status:** The official state death toll as of January 2022 was 246 across 77 counties. A BuzzFeed News analysis using excess mortality data estimated 702 (range: 426-978). Both figures are from credible sources using different methodologies.
**Resolution:** The script appropriately presents both figures with hedging. Verified. No change needed.

### 3. Ninety Thousand Workers -- NYC or National?
**Script (Ch02, line 102):** "the U.S. ice trade employs ninety thousand workers and twenty-five thousand horses, nationally"
**Status:** Some sources ambiguously suggest this figure refers to NYC alone, while others (Wikipedia ice trade, Antique Ice Tool Museum) specify it as a national figure. The research files flag this explicitly.
**Resolution:** The script already specifies "nationally," which is the best-sourced interpretation. Verified. No change needed.

## Methodology Notes

- All script claims were first cross-referenced against the research files (overview.md and chapter-specific research files).

- Particular attention was paid to attributed quotes, as these are the highest-risk items for AI fabrication. No fabricated quotes were found.
- The date range error on COVID vaccine waste (the most significant factual error found) was corrected in the script.

- Where claims could not be independently verified, hedging language was added or the existing hedging was confirmed as adequate.
