This is a kid who rejected Harvard at thirteen, by the way. Told his father formal education was a waste of time.

JEFF: From frozen pond water. And that arc... from madman to mogul, from a joke in the Boston Gazette to a two hundred million dollar fortune... that's just the beginning of what we're going to talk about today.

--- SEGMENT BREAK: Opening - By the Numbers ---

JEFF: Hit me.

CYRUS: Okay, here's one. In nineteen thirty... eight percent of American homes had a refrigerator. Eight percent. By nineteen forty-four, eighty-five percent did. That's one of the fastest technology adoptions in American history. And it happened during the Great Depression and World War Two.

JEFF: During the Depression. When nobody has money. That's when America decides it can't live without a fridge.

JEFF: More than lumber. More than grain. Second only to cotton.

CYRUS: Second only to cotton. And today, here's where it gets really big. Refrigeration and air conditioning consume twenty-five to thirty percent of all global electricity. A quarter of every power plant on Earth runs to keep things cold. Nothing else in your house comes close.

JEFF: Think about that. Every fourth light bulb, every fourth factory, every fourth anything... is just keeping stuff cold.

CYRUS: Seventy percent of all food consumed worldwide passes through the cold chain at some point. Not just frozen food... fresh produce, dairy, meat, medicine. It's the invisible conveyor belt feeding the planet.

JEFF: So what happens when the conveyor belt breaks?

CYRUS: That's the other side of the coin. Thirteen percent of all food produced globally is lost to inadequate refrigeration. Enough food to feed nearly a billion people, just... gone. JEFF: [pause] So we've got this technology that touches everything... food, medicine, where people live, how much energy we consume. And it went from a joke on a Boston dock to the invisible backbone of modern civilization in about two hundred years.

CYRUS: That's exactly right. And the story of HOW it got there... that's what today is about.

--- SEGMENT BREAK: Opening - The World Before ---

CYRUS: Half of all children in the city die before age five. And a primary killer... is milk.

JEFF: Milk.

CYRUS: Milk. So here's what's happening. Across Manhattan and Brooklyn, large distilleries are running a side business. The leftover mash from whiskey production. That's all they eat.

JEFF: That's... that can't be good for the cows.

CYRUS: The cows never go outside. They develop open sores. Their tails rot and fall off from disease. Many lose their teeth. They produce milk until they literally drop dead, and their carcasses are dragged away.

JEFF: [pause] And people are drinking this milk?

Flour and starch to thicken it. They mix in eggs, Plaster of Paris, water. A dash of molasses to sweeten it. What mothers are buying for their babies is a cup of poison disguised as nourishment.

JEFF: That is horrifying.

CYRUS: Between fifty and eighty percent of all milk sold in major northeastern cities is this swill milk. An estimated eight thousand babies die per year in New York City alone from contaminated milk. Eight thousand.

JEFF: Per YEAR?

CYRUS: Per year. And in eighteen fifty-eight, a publisher named Frank Leslie runs an explosive expose in his Illustrated Newspaper. Five thousand words. Illustrations of the conditions... cows standing in filth, tails rotted off, maps showing which dairies are the worst offenders. He calls them "milk murderers" distributing "liquid poison." And he asks readers directly... "Are you aware what kind of milk you are drinking?"

JEFF: And there's no way to fix this without cold. Right? That's the connection.

CYRUS: That's exactly the connection. Even honest milk from healthy cows spoils within hours in warm weather. Without refrigeration, the entire milk supply chain is a lottery. And the poorest families, the ones who can only afford the cheapest swill milk at six cents a quart, they bear the consequences.

JEFF: So what were people doing? Just... living without cold?

CYRUS: Daily shopping was mandatory. Meat was purchased the morning it would be eaten. There were no leftovers in any modern sense. Food had to be consumed immediately or it would spoil. Preservation meant jerky, jam, brine, pickling... labor-intensive, imperfect, and it changed the taste of everything. If you had an icebox, it was a wooden cabinet with a block of ice that might last a day. The iceman came every morning, families hung a card in the window saying how many pounds they wanted... twenty-five, fifty, seventy-five, or a hundred. Kids chased the ice wagon for slivers to suck on in the heat.

JEFF: And if you lived in the South?

CYRUS: If you lived in the South, you depended on ice shipped from the North. When the Civil War cut off those shipments... southern cities just went without. Cold was a luxury of geography and season. And it was dangerous to produce. The men and horses harvesting ice worked on frozen lakes in brutal conditions. Workers could fall through thin ice and drown. They could be crushed by four hundred pound blocks. Low wages, backbreaking seasonal labor.

JEFF: [pause] It's a completely different world.

CYRUS: A completely different world. And that's the world we're going to watch disappear over the next ninety minutes.

--- SEGMENT BREAK: Wave 1 - The Ice Trade ---

JEFF: Alright, so you've met the man. You know the joke, the debtor's prison, the Ice Fool. Now let's talk about how he actually pulled it off. Because the story of Tudor's comeback is really a story about three innovations... and none of them are what you'd expect.

CYRUS: None of them are technical.

JEFF: Not a single one. The first is sawdust. Not straw, not blankets, not any of the other insulating materials people had tried... sawdust. Packed around the ice blocks during shipping, sawdust turns out to be remarkably effective at slowing the melt. This isn't some brilliant scientific breakthrough. It's a logistical insight. But it's the insight that makes the whole business viable, because now ice can survive weeks at sea instead of days.

CYRUS: So the difference between a failing business and a global empire is... the packing material.

JEFF: The packing material. And the second discovery is even better. Tudor figures out the demand problem. Remember, people in the tropics don't know what to do with ice. They've never had it. So Tudor starts giving ice away free to bartenders and tavern owners.

CYRUS: Free?

JEFF: Free. Here's what he wrote... "A man who has drank his drinks cold at the same expense for one week can never be presented with them warm again." Think about that. He understood addiction economics before anyone had a word for it. Give them a free taste, and they'll pay for the next one. And the one after that. And the one after that.

CYRUS: That's basically the playbook every tech company uses now. Free trials, freemium models... Frederic Tudor invented the free trial in eighteen fifteen. With ice.

JEFF: With ice. But he still needs to solve the supply side. He's cutting ice blocks by hand, loading them onto ships, and the costs are enormous. And this is where a guy named Nathaniel Wyeth enters the picture.

CYRUS: Tell me about Wyeth.

JEFF: Nathaniel Wyeth. He's a Cambridge hotel owner, runs the Fresh Pond Hotel, and he becomes Tudor's foreman at the Fresh Pond ice-harvesting operation. And around eighteen twenty-five, Wyeth invents something that transforms the entire economics of ice. The horse-drawn ice plow.

CYRUS: Okay, describe this for me.

JEFF: It's beautifully simple. Two parallel iron runners, twenty inches apart, notched with saw teeth, drawn by a horse across the frozen surface. It scores the ice in uniform grids, producing clean, regular blocks that pack tightly in ships and store efficiently. Before Wyeth's plow, ice cost thirty cents a ton to harvest. After? Ten cents.

CYRUS: He tripled the efficiency.

JEFF: Tripled. And cut costs by two thirds. Now you've got three things... sawdust insulation that keeps the ice from melting, Wyeth's plow that makes harvesting cheap and scalable, and Tudor's marketing genius that creates demand. THAT is the combination that turns a "slippery speculation" into a global empire.

CYRUS: And history remembers Tudor. But Wyeth...

JEFF: Wyeth remained greatly out of the spotlight. He's the guy who made Tudor's vision physically possible, and almost nobody knows his name. He kept writing Tudor letters, urging him not to neglect the ice business. He never achieved Tudor's fame or fortune. He died in eighteen fifty-six, the last connection to the founding era of the industry.

CYRUS: It's the classic story, right? The visionary gets the credit. The engineer who makes it work gets forgotten.

JEFF: Every time.

CYRUS: Calcutta.

JEFF: Calcutta. He's going to ship ice to India. Sixteen thousand miles. Four months at sea. Across two oceans, through tropical heat the entire way. Most people think it's an elaborate joke. Ice, shipped from the other side of the world? Impossible.

CYRUS: And for four months, that ice is just sitting in the hold, in tropical heat, slowly melting.

CYRUS: So what happens when they open the hold?

JEFF: [pause] They can't unload right away. There's paperwork. The customs papers take time, and the captain isn't allowed to start unloading until sunset. The crowd is waiting. CYRUS: A hundred tons! After four months!

JEFF: A hundred tons of frozen Massachusetts pond water, intact, in Calcutta, India. Observers describe the blocks as "crystal blocks of Yankee coldness." One Indian onlooker touches a block and thinks he's been BURNT. Another asks Captain Littlefield whether ice grows on trees in America.

CYRUS: [laughs] Whether ice grows on trees!

And within three days, Calcutta's British residents raise enough money to commission a proper ice house. Every day, the proprietors display a huge block of shimmering ice to passersby from behind a glass window, replacing it with fresh blocks regularly. It becomes a spectacle. People come just to LOOK at it.

CYRUS: And does it make money?

JEFF: Over the next twenty years, Calcutta becomes Tudor's most profitable destination. He eventually builds three ice houses in India... Calcutta, Bombay, and Madras. In Bombay, a merchant named Jamshetji Jeejeebhoy becomes the first person to serve ice cream at dinner parties using Tudor's ice, though the newspaper notes that some guests caught colds, which the Bombay paper deems... and I love this... "a worthy price to pay."

CYRUS: [laughs] A worthy price to pay for ice cream.

JEFF: Henry David Thoreau, watching ice harvesters at Walden Pond, wrote one of the most beautiful passages about this whole trade. He said... CYRUS: [pause] That's extraordinary. I think what gets me is the audacity of the SCALE. A contemporary journal wrote that "the arrival of the Tuscany with a cargo of ice from America forms an epoch in the history of Calcutta worthy of commemoration."

JEFF: An epoch. That's what they called it. This is a guy who started with a joke at a family dinner, went to debtor's prison, and ended up changing how an entire subcontinent experiences food and drink.

CYRUS: And once they had it...

JEFF: They could never go back. And THAT is the pattern. That's the engine of this entire story. You get people to taste cold... and they're hooked. Tudor understood that before anyone.

--- SEGMENT BREAK: Wave 1 - Diffusion and the Empire's Peak ---

CYRUS: So what does the ice industry look like at its peak? Because we've been focused on Tudor, but this thing gets enormous.

JEFF: Enormous. By the eighteen fifties, ice delivery is no longer a luxury in American cities. It's a daily expectation. Two-thirds of households in Boston and New York receive ice every single day. Fifteen hundred ice trucks are making daily rounds in New York City alone. Children chasing the ice wagon for slivers... that's a daily ritual in every neighborhood.

CYRUS: And the industry behind all of this...

JEFF: By the eighteen eighties, the U.S. ice trade employs ninety thousand workers and twenty-five thousand horses, nationally. The industry is capitalized at twenty-eight million dollars, which is over six hundred million in today's money. There are a hundred and thirty-five major ice warehouses along the Hudson River between Poughkeepsie and Albany. American ships are carrying more tonnage of ice than any commodity except cotton. It is the second-largest shipping industry in America, and the entire thing is built on frozen pond water.

CYRUS: That's sixty years from a laughingstock on a dock to the second-biggest shipping industry in America. From one ship to ninety thousand workers.

JEFF: And the tipping point wasn't any single dramatic moment. It was the quiet normalization of cold. Somewhere in the eighteen fifties, cold drinks stopped being a luxury and became just... what you expected. Tudor was right. Once you've tasted cold, you can't go back.

CYRUS: But there's a problem lurking in all of this, isn't there?

JEFF: A fatal one. The entire empire is built on one assumption.

CYRUS: That winter will freeze the rivers.

JEFF: That winter will freeze the rivers.

--- SEGMENT BREAK: Wave 1 - Anchor Story: The Ice Famine of 1890 ---

JEFF: It's the winter of eighteen eighty-nine to eighteen ninety. And across the northeastern United States... winter doesn't really show up.

CYRUS: How warm are we talking?

JEFF: January eighteen ninety records some of the highest temperatures ever measured in parts of the region. The Hudson River ice harvest... the one that feeds New York City's three-million-ton annual appetite... fails entirely. The great ice fields that are supposed to form between Poughkeepsie and Albany simply don't.

CYRUS: Three million tons per year. And the source just... doesn't produce.

JEFF: Doesn't produce. The Knickerbocker Ice Company, New York's dominant supplier, scrambles. But transportation costs from Maine are enormous, and the supply falls far short of what New York needs.

CYRUS: So what happens to prices?

JEFF: They skyrocket. Ice that had been affordable hits ten dollars a ton in New York. Twenty dollars a ton in Cincinnati. And do you know why?

CYRUS: The South has ice machines.

JEFF: The South has ice machines! The region that had always depended on northern ice shipments has been investing in artificial ice-making. So when the warm winter hits, the South is actually better prepared than the North. The irony is incredible.

CYRUS: That IS ironic. So who gets hurt the worst?

JEFF: The urban poor. Working families who can only afford to buy ice in small daily quantities, like buying coal by the bucket, they face the steepest price increases. Hospitals are giving out ice for free during heat waves, and at least once, it leads to what sources describe as an "ice riot," with crowds fighting for limited supplies.

CYRUS: An ice riot. In the world's most modern city. Over frozen water.

JEFF: Over frozen water. And this is the moment when people realize the whole system is fragile.

CYRUS: Right. The Ice Famine of eighteen ninety doesn't kill the natural ice industry overnight. But it exposes the fatal flaw that everyone should have seen from the beginning. JEFF: And it's not just the warm winters. The ice itself is getting dirty.

CYRUS: Tell me about that.

JEFF: The Hudson River, where most of New York's ice comes from, is increasingly fouled with industrial waste and sewage. Between eighteen ninety-eight and nineteen oh seven, hundreds of New Yorkers die from typhoid, with cases in the thousands. By nineteen oh seven, specialists declare Hudson River ice unsafe for consumption. In nineteen oh nine, the state condemns forty-one ice houses around Albany.

CYRUS: So the natural ice industry's biggest argument... "Our ice is natural, it's pure, it comes from nature"...

JEFF: Collapses. Because by nineteen hundred, "natural" ice means ice laced with sewage and typhoid. The industry that had attacked inventors for producing "unnatural" ice is now selling ice that could literally give you typhoid fever. Artificial ice, made from filtered and treated water, is now the CLEANER option.

CYRUS: The irony is complete.

JEFF: The irony is complete. By nineteen fourteen, artificial ice production surpasses natural ice in the United States... twenty-six million tons artificial versus twenty-four million tons natural. CYRUS: And those ninety thousand workers... what happens to them?

JEFF: [pause] You know what's striking? We don't really know. An entire workforce, and the historical record is almost silent. It just... dried up. Ice warehouses along the Hudson were abandoned, some eventually destroyed by lightning after sitting derelict for years. And the communities that had thrived on ice harvesting gradually lost that piece of their economic base. There were no retraining programs, no organized assistance. They just vanish from the record.

CYRUS: An entire industry, and they just... vanish.

JEFF: They vanish. And the world moves on. Because the question now isn't whether people want cold. Tudor proved that beyond any doubt. The question is whether nature can keep providing it. And that someone... is a doctor in Florida, treating malaria patients, who becomes obsessed with cooling sickrooms.

CYRUS: His name is John Gorrie.

JEFF: His name is John Gorrie. And his story is one of the most tragic in the history of American invention. Cyrus, this is your wave. Take it away.

--- SEGMENT BREAK: Wave 2 - The Machine Age ---

CYRUS: So the world now expects cold. Tudor proved the market. Wyeth proved the economics. And then nature said... no guarantees. The stage is set for somebody to figure out how to make cold without nature. And the person who does it first... his story is one of the most tragic things I've come across in all the research for this episode.

JEFF: John Gorrie.

CYRUS: John Gorrie. It's the eighteen forties. Apalachicola, Florida. At this point, Apalachicola is the third-largest port on the Gulf Coast. A boomtown built on cotton. JEFF: A doctor with a lot of side gigs.

CYRUS: A LOT of side gigs. But Apalachicola also suffers terribly from malaria and yellow fever. And remember, this is the eighteen forties. The miasma theory of disease is dominant... people believe that bad air, foul-smelling air, causes illness. Gorrie becomes convinced that if he can just cool the air in his patients' sickrooms, he can cure them.

JEFF: So he's basically imagining air conditioning... decades before anyone has a word for it.

CYRUS: Exactly. He starts by hanging basins of ice from the ceiling of hospital rooms, letting cool air sink down over his feverish patients. And it actually helps. Cooler rooms, patients feel better. But ice in Florida is expensive and unreliable. It all has to be shipped from the North. So Gorrie starts asking a radical question... what if you could make ice without nature? What if a machine could produce cold?

JEFF: And he builds one.

CYRUS: He builds one. Around eighteen forty-four, Gorrie gives up his medical practice... his entire livelihood as the leading physician in a prosperous port town... to pursue this ice-making machine full time. Think about what a sacrifice that is. He walks away from a successful career to chase an invention.

JEFF: That takes a particular kind of obsession.

CYRUS: His machine works on the principle of vapor compression. Compress a gas, cool it, let it expand rapidly, and the expansion produces intense cold. And here's the thing that's going to hit you, Jeff... that is the exact same principle that powers every refrigerator and every air conditioner on Earth today. Gorrie figured it out in eighteen forty-four.

JEFF: In Apalachicola, Florida.

CYRUS: In Apalachicola, Florida. On May sixth, eighteen fifty-one, he receives U.S. Patent Number eight oh eight oh for... and I'm quoting... "an improved process for the artificial production of ice." He demonstrates the machine publicly. Actual ice, produced mechanically. People are astonished.

JEFF: So... triumph?

CYRUS: [pause] For about five minutes.

JEFF: Oh no.

CYRUS: The northern ice industry sees Gorrie's machine as an existential threat. Remember that empire we just talked about... the one with all those warehouses along the Hudson and all those ships? They've got a massively profitable monopoly. And this doctor in Florida is threatening to make it all irrelevant. So the northern newspapers attack Gorrie. Viciously. A New York newspaper publishes what becomes the most notorious line... "There is a crank down in Apalachicola, Florida, that thinks he can make ice by his machine as good as God Almighty."

JEFF: "As good as God Almighty." So they're framing it as... hubris? Man versus nature?

CYRUS: Man versus nature, and man versus God. The ice industry's argument is almost theological. Nature makes ice. Nature makes it pure. Who is this man to think he can do what nature does? And Gorrie himself suspected that the Ice King's successors were personally behind the press attacks... that the northern ice syndicate had orchestrated a smear campaign to protect their monopoly.

JEFF: Was there any proof?

CYRUS: We know the northern ice syndicate saw Gorrie's invention as a threat to their cartel and set out to discredit him in the press. Whether specific individuals pulled the strings... Gorrie believed it, but we can't confirm it.

JEFF: And here's what I think is important... the ice industry's resistance wasn't PURELY cynical. Right? There's a real cultural anxiety here.

CYRUS: That's exactly right. People genuinely trusted "natural" ice more than machine-made ice. Just like today, people trust "natural" and "organic" food over processed food. In the eighteen fifties, the idea that a machine could produce something as pure as what nature provides... that felt wrong to a lot of people. The resisters weren't all villains. They were speaking to a real human instinct.

JEFF: Which makes what happens next even more painful.

CYRUS: In eighteen fifty-one... the same year Gorrie gets his patent... his main financial backer dies. Gone. And with the press attacking him relentlessly, no other investors will step forward. Nobody wants to fund the machine that the newspapers are calling blasphemous. Gorrie's finances collapse. He's sued by a London debt collector for over six thousand five hundred dollars in unpaid interest... a crushing sum in that era. He suffers what sources describe as "a nervous breakdown from which he never recovered."

JEFF: [pause] And?

CYRUS: On June twenty-ninth, eighteen fifty-five, John Gorrie dies in seclusion in Apalachicola. He's fifty-one years old. He's survived by his wife Caroline and two children. He dies without ever having profited from the invention that would change the world.

JEFF: [sigh] The man who invented the fundamental technology behind every refrigerator and air conditioner on Earth... dies broke and broken at fifty-one.

CYRUS: And today... his statue stands in the U.S. Capitol. He's one of Florida's two representatives in the National Statuary Hall. A museum in Apalachicola houses a replica of his machine. But he never saw any of it.

JEFF: It's one of those stories where you just want to reach back through time and tell him... you were right. You WERE right.

CYRUS: He was right. And the ice industry was wrong. But they didn't know that yet. And that's the tension that runs through this whole wave... when is resistance reasonable caution, and when is it just protecting a monopoly?

--- SEGMENT BREAK: Wave 2 - How It Works ---

JEFF: So, Cyrus, before we move on... I think this is a good moment to talk about how refrigeration actually works. Because Gorrie figured out the basic principle, and we've been saying "compress a gas, let it expand." But what does that actually mean?

CYRUS: Yeah, let's break it down. And I promise I'll keep this short. So here's the key insight, and it's counterintuitive. A refrigerator doesn't make cold. It PUMPS heat. It takes the heat from inside the box and moves it to the outside.

JEFF: Wait, it doesn't create cold?

CYRUS: It does not create cold. It removes warmth. Think of it this way. Inside the fridge, there's a special fluid called a refrigerant flowing through a closed loop. When that fluid evaporates inside the fridge, it absorbs heat from your food. The inside gets cooler. The fluid becomes a gas. Then a compressor squeezes that gas, which heats it up. The hot gas passes through the coils on the back of the fridge... those warm coils you can feel if you touch the back... and it dumps that heat into your kitchen. The gas cools down, condenses back into a liquid, flows back inside, and the whole cycle repeats.

JEFF: So my fridge is literally taking warmth out of my food and dumping it into my kitchen.

CYRUS: Exactly. Your kitchen is slightly warmer because your fridge is running. It's a heat pump.

JEFF: Okay, but what's the analogy? What's the thing that makes this click?

CYRUS: Sweating. Your fridge works exactly like sweating. When sweat evaporates off your skin, it absorbs heat and cools you down. A refrigerator does the same thing with a special fluid in a closed loop. The only difference is... your body sweats into the air, and a fridge sweats into its coils.

JEFF: Huh. So when I'm standing in front of the open fridge on a hot day...

CYRUS: You're basically standing in front of a machine that sweats. [laughs] And that's Gorrie's insight. Compress a gas, let it expand, and the expansion absorbs heat... produces cold. Every fridge, every air conditioner, every freezer on the planet uses that same basic idea. A doctor in Apalachicola, Florida, figured it out in eighteen forty-four.

JEFF: Got it. So the principle works. But it takes a while for anyone to make it commercially viable.

--- SEGMENT BREAK: Wave 2 - Linde and the Breweries ---

CYRUS: It takes decades. And the person who finally cracks it is a German professor named Carl von Linde. And he succeeds for a reason that's worth understanding... he finds the right market.

JEFF: Which is?

CYRUS: Beer. Specifically, lager. So here's the thing about lager. Unlike ale, lager requires consistent cold temperatures during fermentation. Bavarian breweries needed cold to survive, and they could only brew half the year.

JEFF: So for breweries, refrigeration isn't a nice-to-have. It's a...

CYRUS: Competitive necessity. And in eighteen seventy-three, Linde installs his first ammonia-based refrigerating machine at the Spaten Brewery in Munich. It works. It works RELIABLY. And suddenly the Spaten Brewery can produce lager year-round. The summer brewing ban becomes irrelevant.

JEFF: How quickly does this spread?

CYRUS: By eighteen ninety, Linde has sold seven hundred and forty-seven machines, primarily to breweries. He founds a company, Linde AG, in eighteen seventy-nine. That company still exists today. JEFF: [laughs] I love that.

CYRUS: So Linde proves that mechanical refrigeration works at industrial scale. It's reliable. It's economical. But the story of HOW it spreads... that's where it gets really interesting. Because the next guy in this story doesn't just adopt refrigeration. He uses it to blow up an entire industry.

--- SEGMENT BREAK: Wave 2 - Anchor Story: Swift vs. the Railroads and the Butchers ---

No mechanical parts needed. Just physics.

JEFF: Smart.

CYRUS: Very smart. Now Swift goes to the railroads. "Here's my refrigerated car. Will you carry my dressed beef from Chicago to the East Coast?"

JEFF: And let me guess. They say no.

JEFF: That's the classic innovator's dilemma, right? The incumbent has too much invested in the old way.

JEFF: And... is that a fair point?

JEFF: So the resisters have a point.

CYRUS: The resisters ALWAYS have a point. That's what makes these stories interesting. But Swift fights back, and he fights dirty. He undercuts local prices. It was brutal.

JEFF: And Swift wins.

CYRUS: Within six years of launching his refrigerated rail line, Gustavus Swift is the second-largest meatpacker in America. Chicago becomes the meatpacking capital of the world. JEFF: The Jungle.

CYRUS: The Jungle. Upton Sinclair, nineteen oh six. The hellish conditions of centralized meatpacking that Sinclair documented... those conditions are a DIRECT consequence of the system Swift built. JEFF: Every solution creates a new problem. We keep seeing that.

CYRUS: And meanwhile, the same story is playing out internationally. In eighteen eighty, the SS Strathleven arrives in London carrying fifty tons of frozen beef and mutton from Australia. JEFF: So by the turn of the century, mechanical refrigeration is dominating industry. Breweries, meatpacking, cold storage, railroad cars, international shipping...

CYRUS: All mechanical. All running on manufactured cold. The transition from natural to artificial ice is effectively complete at the industrial level.

JEFF: But...

CYRUS: But it doesn't work in homes.

JEFF: Right. Because the machines are huge. They're expensive. And they run on...

JEFF: So people who bought early home refrigerators...

JEFF: Albert Einstein designed a refrigerator?

CYRUS: Albert Einstein designed a refrigerator. Motivated by a dead family in Berlin. They patent it in nineteen thirty. But by then, someone else has already found a very different solution to the problem. Getting cold into the factory was an engineering challenge. Getting cold into the kitchen... that turns out to be a chemistry problem. And the chemist who solves it is about to walk onto a stage and do something absolutely unforgettable.

JEFF: That's my wave. Let's go.
