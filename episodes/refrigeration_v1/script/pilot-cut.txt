--- SEGMENT: The Cold Open ---

JEFF: Picture this. It's February, eighteen oh six. Boston harbor. A twenty-three year old kid from one of the city's best families is standing on the dock, watching workers load a hundred and thirty tons of frozen pond water onto a ship called the Favorite. Ice. Literal blocks of ice, cut from a pond in Saugus, Massachusetts, bound for the Caribbean island of Martinique.

CYRUS: [laughs softly] Okay, I need to stop you right there. Frozen pond water. On a ship. To the Caribbean.

JEFF: To the Caribbean. And here's the thing... no ship in Boston would carry the cargo. Captain after captain turned him down. They were convinced the melting ice would sink their vessel. This guy, Frederic Tudor, twenty-three years old, had to buy the ship himself just to get his ice out of the harbor.

CYRUS: So how did this even start? What makes a twenty-three year old from a wealthy Boston family decide his life's purpose is... shipping ice to the tropics?

JEFF: A joke. Literally a joke. His brother William cracks wise at a family gathering in eighteen oh five about selling ice to the West Indies. Everybody laughs. Frederic doesn't laugh. He goes home, starts writing feverishly in his diary, calculating shipping routes, studying insulation methods. His family thinks he's lost his mind. This is a kid who rejected Harvard at thirteen, by the way. Told his father formal education was a waste of time.

CYRUS: Bold move.

JEFF: Bold is one word for it. So the day the Favorite sets sail, the Boston Gazette runs it as a joke. They literally print... "No JOKE. A vessel has cleared at the Custom House for Martinique with a cargo of ice. We hope this will not prove a slippery speculation."

CYRUS: "Slippery speculation." [laughs] That's pretty good, actually.

JEFF: Pretty good. And also... pretty accurate. Because that first voyage? Disaster. Most of the ice melts on the way down. Tudor shows up in Martinique with a fraction of what he loaded, and here's the kicker... nobody there knows what to do with it. They've never HAD ice. There's no market. He has to literally teach people what ice is for.

CYRUS: Wait... so he shipped a product thousands of miles to a place where nobody wanted it, and most of it melted on the way. That is not a great business plan.

JEFF: Terrible business plan. He went broke. He went to debtor's prison at least twice. The years between eighteen oh nine and eighteen thirteen, he's in and out of jail, hiding from the sheriff. They called him "the Ice Fool."

CYRUS: But he kept going.

JEFF: He kept going. He wrote in his diary during this period... "He who gives back at the first repulse and without striking the second blow despairs of success, has never been, is not, and never will be a hero in war, love, or business."

CYRUS: [pause] And how does the story end?

JEFF: Frederic Tudor dies in eighteen sixty-four worth the equivalent of roughly two hundred million dollars in today's money. He created an entirely new global industry out of something Bostonians scraped off frozen ponds and considered worthless. From Ice Fool... to Ice King.

CYRUS: Two hundred million dollars. From frozen pond water.

--- SEGMENT: By the Numbers ---

CYRUS: Let me put some numbers on this. Because the refrigeration story isn't just about one eccentric Bostonian. This is one of the biggest transformations in the history of human civilization, and it shows up in the numbers in ways that are genuinely shocking.

JEFF: Hit me.

CYRUS: In nineteen thirty... eight percent of American homes had a refrigerator. Eight percent. By nineteen forty-four, eighty-five percent did. That's one of the fastest technology adoptions in American history. And it happened during the Great Depression and World War Two.

JEFF: During the Depression. When nobody has money. That's when America decides it can't live without a fridge.

CYRUS: Here's another one. Before the Civil War, American ships carried more tonnage of ice than any commodity except cotton. An entire global shipping industry... built on frozen pond water.

JEFF: More than lumber. More than grain. Second only to cotton.

CYRUS: And today... refrigeration and air conditioning consume twenty-five to thirty percent of all global electricity. A quarter of every power plant on Earth runs to keep things cold. Seventy percent of all food consumed worldwide passes through the cold chain at some point. Not just frozen food... fresh produce, dairy, meat, medicine. It's the invisible conveyor belt feeding the planet.

JEFF: So what happens when the conveyor belt breaks?

CYRUS: Thirteen percent of all food produced globally is lost to inadequate refrigeration. Enough food to feed nearly a billion people, just... gone. Because a billion people on this planet still can't keep food cold. And the cost is measured in hunger.

--- SEGMENT: The World Before ---

JEFF: I think we need to talk about what life was actually like before refrigeration. Because people hear "before refrigerators" and picture a slightly less convenient kitchen. The reality is so much worse.

CYRUS: Let me take you to New York City, the eighteen fifties. For babies born in Manhattan... half of them won't make it to their fifth birthday.

JEFF: Half.

CYRUS: Half of all children die before age five. And a primary killer... is milk.

JEFF: Milk.

CYRUS: So here's what's happening. Across Manhattan and Brooklyn, large distilleries are running a side business. They've got cows — hundreds of them — penned shoulder to shoulder in sheds attached directly to the distillery. And these cows are fed nothing but hot distillery slop. The leftover mash from whiskey production. The cows never go outside. They develop open sores. Their tails rot and fall off from disease. Many lose their teeth. They produce milk until they literally drop dead.

JEFF: And people are drinking this milk?

CYRUS: The milk these diseased cows produce is thin, bluish, foul. But the vendors don't sell it that way. They doctor it. They add chalk to whiten it. Flour and starch to thicken it. Eggs, Plaster of Paris, water. A dash of molasses to sweeten it. What mothers are buying for their babies is a cup of poison disguised as nourishment.

JEFF: That is horrifying.

CYRUS: Between fifty and eighty percent of all milk sold in major northeastern cities is this swill milk. An estimated eight thousand babies die per year in New York City alone from contaminated milk.

JEFF: Per YEAR?

CYRUS: Per year. Even honest milk from healthy cows spoils within hours in warm weather. Without refrigeration, the entire milk supply chain is a lottery. Daily shopping was mandatory. Meat was purchased the morning it would be eaten. There were no leftovers in any modern sense. If you had an icebox, it was a wooden cabinet with a block of ice that might last a day. The iceman came every morning. Kids chased the ice wagon for slivers to suck on in the heat.

JEFF: [pause] It's a completely different world.

CYRUS: A completely different world.

--- SEGMENT: Tudor's Playbook ---

JEFF: Alright, so you've met the man. You know the joke, the debtor's prison, the Ice Fool. Now let's talk about how he actually pulled it off. Because the story of Tudor's comeback is really a story about three innovations... and none of them are what you'd expect.

CYRUS: None of them are technical.

JEFF: Not a single one. The first is sawdust. Not straw, not blankets, not any of the other insulating materials people had tried... sawdust. Packed around the ice blocks during shipping, it turns out to be remarkably effective at slowing the melt. It's a logistical insight. And it makes the whole business viable.

CYRUS: The difference between a failing business and a global empire is... the packing material.

JEFF: The packing material. And the second discovery is even better. Tudor figures out the demand problem. People in the tropics don't know what to do with ice. They've never had it. So Tudor starts giving ice away free to bartenders and tavern owners.

CYRUS: Free?

JEFF: Free. Here's what he wrote... "A man who has drank his drinks cold at the same expense for one week can never be presented with them warm again." He understood addiction economics before anyone had a word for it. Give them a free taste, and they'll pay for the next one.

CYRUS: Frederic Tudor invented the free trial in eighteen fifteen. With ice.

JEFF: With ice. And the third innovation... he needed to solve the supply side. A Cambridge hotel owner named Nathaniel Wyeth becomes his foreman at the ice-harvesting operation, and around eighteen twenty-five, Wyeth invents the horse-drawn ice plow. Two parallel iron runners, notched with saw teeth, drawn across the frozen surface. It scores the ice in uniform grids, clean regular blocks that pack tightly. Before Wyeth's plow, ice cost thirty cents a ton to harvest. After? Ten cents. He tripled the efficiency.

Now you've got three things... sawdust insulation, Wyeth's plow that makes harvesting scalable, and Tudor's marketing genius that creates demand. THAT is the combination that turns a "slippery speculation" into a global empire.

CYRUS: And history remembers Tudor. But Wyeth...

JEFF: Wyeth remained greatly out of the spotlight. He's the guy who made Tudor's vision physically possible, and almost nobody knows his name.

CYRUS: The visionary gets the credit. The engineer who makes it work gets forgotten.

JEFF: Every time.

--- SEGMENT: Calcutta ---

JEFF: With Wyeth's plow and sawdust, Tudor can scale. He conquers the Caribbean. He conquers the American South. But in eighteen thirty-three, he makes his most audacious bet.

CYRUS: Calcutta.

JEFF: Calcutta. Sixteen thousand miles. Four months at sea. Across two oceans, through tropical heat the entire way. On May twelfth, eighteen thirty-three, the brig Tuscany sets sail from Boston. In the hold... one hundred and eighty tons of New England ice, packed in sawdust. The route takes the ship south along the Atlantic, around the Cape of Good Hope, and across the Indian Ocean.

CYRUS: For four months, that ice is just sitting in the hold, slowly melting.

JEFF: Four months. Seven days. And on September thirteenth, eighteen thirty-three, the Tuscany approaches Calcutta. Word has spread through the city. Crowds gather at the port. Many believe it's a hoax... how could ice possibly survive a voyage from the other side of the world? Finally, when the blocks are brought ashore... of the original hundred and eighty tons, roughly a hundred tons have survived.

CYRUS: A hundred tons! After four months!

JEFF: A hundred tons of frozen Massachusetts pond water, intact, in Calcutta, India. Observers describe the blocks as "crystal blocks of Yankee coldness." One onlooker touches a block and thinks he's been BURNT. Another asks the captain whether ice grows on trees in America.

CYRUS: [laughs] Whether ice grows on trees!

JEFF: The government lets the ice land duty-free. Waives customs formalities. The ice is rushed to a makeshift storage room and every day, the proprietors display a huge block of shimmering ice to passersby from behind a glass window. It becomes a spectacle. People come just to LOOK at it.

Over the next twenty years, Calcutta becomes Tudor's most profitable destination. Henry David Thoreau, watching ice harvesters at Walden Pond, wrote... "The sweltering inhabitants of Charleston and New Orleans, of Madras and Bombay and Calcutta, drink at my well. The pure Walden water is mingled with the sacred water of the Ganges."

CYRUS: [pause] A contemporary journal called it "an epoch in the history of Calcutta worthy of commemoration." An epoch. For ice.

JEFF: This is a guy who started with a joke at a family dinner, went to debtor's prison, and ended up changing how an entire subcontinent experiences food and drink.

CYRUS: And once they had it...

JEFF: They could never go back. And THAT is the pattern. You get people to taste cold... and they're hooked. Tudor understood that before anyone.

--- SEGMENT: The Ice Famine ---

CYRUS: But the whole empire is built on one assumption.

JEFF: That winter will freeze the rivers.

CYRUS: It's the winter of eighteen eighty-nine to eighteen ninety. And winter doesn't really show up. The Hudson River ice harvest — the one that feeds New York City's three-million-ton annual appetite — fails entirely. Prices skyrocket. Ice hits ten dollars a ton in New York, twenty dollars in Cincinnati. And here's a detail I love... a Savannah, Georgia newspaper smugly notes that THEIR ice prices are much lower, despite ninety-degree days.

JEFF: The South has ice machines.

CYRUS: The South has ice machines! The region that had always depended on northern ice shipments had been investing in artificial ice-making. When the warm winter hits, the South is actually better prepared than the North.

JEFF: Who gets hurt the worst?

CYRUS: The urban poor. Working families who can only buy ice in small daily quantities face the steepest price increases. Hospitals give out ice for free during heat waves. Sources describe what they call an "ice riot," with crowds fighting for limited supplies.

JEFF: An ice riot. In the world's most modern city. Over frozen water.

CYRUS: And it's not just the warm winters. The Hudson River is increasingly fouled with industrial waste and sewage. By nineteen oh seven, specialists declare Hudson River ice unsafe for consumption. The natural ice industry's biggest argument — "our ice is natural, it's pure" —

JEFF: Collapses. Because "natural" ice now means ice laced with sewage and typhoid. Artificial ice, made from filtered water, is now the CLEANER option.

CYRUS: By nineteen fourteen, artificial ice production surpasses natural ice. And those ninety thousand workers...

JEFF: We don't really know. Most were seasonal — farmers in their off-season, sailors seeking winter income. They didn't lose a full-time job. They lost a seasonal income stream. It dried up. Ice warehouses along the Hudson were abandoned. Those communities just vanish from the record.

CYRUS: And the world moves on. Because someone needs to build a machine that makes cold on demand, regardless of the weather. And that someone is a doctor in Florida, treating malaria patients.

JEFF: His name is John Gorrie.

--- SEGMENT: John Gorrie ---

CYRUS: It's the eighteen forties. Apalachicola, Florida. John Gorrie is the town's most prominent citizen — physician, postmaster, city councilman, bank director. Apalachicola suffers terribly from malaria and yellow fever.

JEFF: And the miasma theory of disease is dominant. People believe bad air causes illness.

CYRUS: Gorrie becomes convinced that if he can cool the air in his patients' sickrooms, he can cure them. He starts by hanging basins of ice from the ceiling of hospital rooms, letting cool air sink down over his feverish patients. And it actually helps. So he asks a radical question... what if you could make ice without nature?

He gives up his medical practice — his entire livelihood — to pursue an ice-making machine full time. Around eighteen forty-four, he builds one. It works on the principle of vapor compression. Compress a gas, cool it, let it expand rapidly, and the expansion produces intense cold. And here's the thing, Jeff... that is the exact same principle that powers every refrigerator and air conditioner on Earth today. Gorrie figured it out in eighteen forty-four.

JEFF: In Apalachicola, Florida.

CYRUS: In Apalachicola. On May sixth, eighteen fifty-one, he receives U.S. Patent Number eight oh eight oh for an "improved process for the artificial production of ice." He demonstrates it publicly. Actual ice, produced mechanically. People are astonished.

JEFF: So... triumph?

CYRUS: [pause] For about five minutes.

JEFF: Oh no.

CYRUS: The northern ice industry sees Gorrie's machine as an existential threat. And the newspapers attack him. Viciously. A New York newspaper publishes what becomes the most notorious line... "There is a crank down in Apalachicola, Florida, that thinks he can make ice by his machine as good as God Almighty."

JEFF: "As good as God Almighty." Man versus nature. Man versus God.

CYRUS: But here's what I think is important. The resistance wasn't purely cynical. People genuinely trusted natural ice more than machine-made ice. Just like today, people trust "natural" and "organic" over processed. In the eighteen fifties, the idea that a machine could produce something as pure as what nature provides... that felt wrong to a lot of people. The resisters weren't all villains. They were speaking to a real human instinct.

JEFF: Which makes what happens next even more painful.

CYRUS: In eighteen fifty-one — the same year Gorrie gets his patent — his main financial backer dies. No other investors will step forward. Nobody wants to fund the machine the newspapers are calling blasphemous. His finances collapse. He suffers what sources describe as "a nervous breakdown from which he never recovered."

On June twenty-ninth, eighteen fifty-five, John Gorrie dies in seclusion in Apalachicola. He's fifty-one years old. He dies without ever having profited from the invention that would change the world.

JEFF: [pause] The man who invented the fundamental technology behind every refrigerator and air conditioner on Earth... dies broke and broken at fifty-one.

CYRUS: Today his statue stands in the U.S. Capitol. A museum in Apalachicola houses a replica of his machine. But he never saw any of it.

JEFF: It's one of those stories where you just want to reach back through time and tell him... you were right.

--- SEGMENT: How It Works (brief) ---

JEFF: This is a good moment to explain how refrigeration actually works. Because Gorrie figured out the basic principle, and we've been saying "compress a gas, let it expand." But what does that actually mean?

CYRUS: Here's the key insight, and it's counterintuitive. A refrigerator doesn't make cold. It PUMPS heat. It takes the heat from inside the box and moves it to the outside.

JEFF: Wait, it doesn't create cold?

CYRUS: It removes warmth. Inside the fridge, there's a refrigerant flowing through a closed loop. When that fluid evaporates inside the fridge, it absorbs heat from your food. The fluid becomes a gas. Then a compressor squeezes that gas, which heats it up. The hot gas passes through the coils on the back of the fridge — those warm coils you can feel — and dumps that heat into your kitchen. The gas cools down, condenses back into a liquid, flows back inside, and the cycle repeats.

JEFF: So my fridge is literally taking warmth out of my food and dumping it into my kitchen.

CYRUS: Your kitchen is slightly warmer because your fridge is running. And the analogy that makes it click... sweating. Your fridge works exactly like sweating. When sweat evaporates off your skin, it absorbs heat and cools you down. A refrigerator does the same thing with a special fluid in a closed loop. Every fridge, every air conditioner, every freezer on the planet. A doctor in Apalachicola figured it out in eighteen forty-four.

--- SEGMENT: Midgley and Freon ---

JEFF: So Gorrie has the principle. A German professor named Linde proves it works at industrial scale — seven hundred and forty-seven machines sold to breweries by eighteen ninety. By the turn of the century, mechanical refrigeration has conquered breweries, meatpacking, railroad cars. But homes? The chemicals running through those machines can kill you in your sleep. Families are keeping refrigerators in the backyard. Einstein is so disturbed by a family in Berlin killed by refrigerant fumes that he spends eight years designing a safer alternative. The world needs a breakthrough. And the man who delivers it... history has complicated feelings about him.

CYRUS: That's one way to put it.

JEFF: His name is Thomas Midgley Junior. Mechanical engineer, working for General Motors and Frigidaire. And by nineteen twenty-eight, he's already infamous — he's the guy who invented tetraethyl lead. Leaded gasoline.

CYRUS: So he's already responsible for one environmental catastrophe.

JEFF: Already responsible for one. But GM's got a problem. Their Frigidaire division is trying to sell home refrigerators, and people keep dying from the fumes. So in nineteen twenty-eight, they hand Midgley a challenge... find us a refrigerant that won't kill our customers.

Midgley and a collaborator named Albert Henne search the periodic table, element by element, looking for compounds that are simultaneously non-toxic, non-flammable, stable, and effective. In three days... three DAYS... they identify dichlorodifluoromethane. A chlorofluorocarbon. They call it Freon.

CYRUS: Three days to find the chemical that changes everything.

JEFF: Three days. And then comes the demonstration. April, nineteen thirty. American Chemical Society meeting, Atlanta, Georgia. Midgley takes the stage. He's carrying a small vial of this new compound and a candle. He lights the candle. Then he does something nobody in that room will ever forget.

He pours the Freon into a bowl. He heats it to a boil with a burner. And then... he deeply inhales a lungful of the vapor.

CYRUS: He BREATHES it?

JEFF: He breathes it. The entire audience is watching. If this chemical is toxic... Midgley is about to collapse on stage. But he doesn't. He stands there perfectly fine. And then he gently exhales the vapor... toward the candle flame.

CYRUS: [pause]

JEFF: The flame goes out.

CYRUS: In one gesture, he proves it's both non-toxic AND non-flammable.

JEFF: In one theatrical gesture. No one in that room can argue Freon is dangerous. Within five years, manufacturers using Freon sell more than eight million refrigerators nationally. The safety crisis is over. The adoption explosion begins. Eight percent of homes with a fridge in nineteen thirty. Eighty-five percent by nineteen forty-four. Faster than smartphones.

CYRUS: And nobody suspects...

JEFF: Nobody suspects a thing.

--- SEGMENT: Frederick McKinley Jones ---

JEFF: So by the late nineteen thirties, refrigeration is in the kitchen. But there's a critical gap. How do you keep food cold in transit? Trucks carry perishables packed in ice and salt, and the ice melts. Long-distance shipments of meat, produce, dairy routinely spoil. The last mile of the cold chain is broken. And the person who fixes it is one of my favorite stories in all the research we've done.

CYRUS: Frederick McKinley Jones.

JEFF: Frederick McKinley Jones. Born May seventeenth, eighteen ninety-three, in Covington, Kentucky. His mother is African American and leaves when Frederick is very young. His father, an Irish railroad worker, struggles to raise him alone and eventually sends him to be raised by a Catholic priest in Cincinnati around age seven. And then when Frederick is nine years old... his father dies. He's an orphan. He leaves school after sixth grade, at age eleven.

CYRUS: [pause] Eleven.

JEFF: Eleven. He gets a job as a cleaning boy at a garage in Cincinnati. And Frederick Jones is one of those people who just... absorbs knowledge. He watches the mechanics. He studies how things work. He's promoted to mechanic's helper. By age fourteen, he's the shop foreman.

CYRUS: Shop foreman at fourteen. With a sixth-grade education.

JEFF: By nineteen, he's one of the most well-known auto racers in the Great Lakes region. He serves as a sergeant in France in World War One, rewires his entire camp for electricity, telephone, and telegraph. After the war, in a small Minnesota town, he teaches himself electronics and builds a radio transmitter for the town.

CYRUS: He left school at eleven. Taught HIMSELF electronics.

JEFF: Around nineteen twenty-seven, a businessman named Joseph Numero is looking for an engineer. Jones shows up. The receptionist tells him... "We don't have any jobs for a colored boy."

CYRUS: [pause]

JEFF: Numero hires him anyway. Jones converts silent-movie projectors into talking projectors using scrap materials. He becomes Numero's indispensable engineer. And then, in nineteen thirty-eight, the moment that changes everything. Numero is on a golf course with a friend in the trucking industry. The friend complains about a recent disaster — a shipment of chickens from St. Paul to Chicago spoiled because the ice and salt couldn't last the trip. Total loss.

Numero bets him six dollars that he has an engineer who can solve the problem in thirty days.

CYRUS: [laughs] Six dollars.

JEFF: Numero goes back to the shop and tells Jones about the bet. And in classic Fred Jones fashion... the man who left school at eleven, who taught himself mechanics, electronics, radio, and cinema sound technology... goes to the library and teaches himself everything about refrigeration. In a matter of weeks, he designs the Thermo Control Model A. A portable, automatic, truck-mounted refrigeration unit.

CYRUS: From a library. In a matter of weeks.

JEFF: Previous attempts had all failed for the same reason... road vibrations broke the equipment. And this is where Jones's background in cinema sound pays off. Sound equipment for movie theaters had to handle vibration without failing. Jones knows how to build for shock. He refines the design, and the Model C, completed in nineteen forty-one, becomes the industry standard. Compact. Lightweight. Shockproof.

CYRUS: All of his previous experience — the garage, the racing, the cinema equipment — it all converges in this one invention.

JEFF: Jones and Numero co-found the U.S. Thermo Control Company. Later renamed Thermo King. Jones is vice president. And then the war comes. The military puts his refrigeration units to work preserving blood plasma, medicine, and food for troops in the field. A six-dollar bet on spoiled chickens ends up helping save soldiers' lives.

Sixty-one patents over his lifetime. In nineteen forty-four, he becomes the first African American elected to the American Society of Refrigeration Engineers.

CYRUS: In nineteen forty-four. During segregation.

JEFF: The man whose job application was met with "We don't have any jobs for a colored boy" becomes the first Black member of the industry's professional society. Frederick McKinley Jones dies on February twenty-first, nineteen sixty-one. In nineteen ninety-one, thirty years after his death, he's posthumously awarded the National Medal of Technology — the first Black American to receive the honor. President George H.W. Bush presents it to Jones's and Numero's widows at a ceremony in the White House Rose Garden.

CYRUS: [pause] Orphaned at nine. Self-taught. Sixty-one patents. Father of refrigerated transportation. And most people have never heard his name.

JEFF: Today, Thermo King is a global leader in transport refrigeration. Without Jones, cold stays in kitchens and warehouses. With Jones... cold moves. The last mile is complete.

--- SEGMENT: The Hidden Cost and Could We Go Back ---

JEFF: So by the nineteen fifties, the picture is complete. The refrigerator is in every kitchen. The supermarket is the new cathedral of abundance. The cold chain stretches from farm to table. Infant mortality has plummeted. Billions of people eating safer food. We live longer because of cold.

And the "safe" chemical making it all possible is silently accumulating in the stratosphere.

Thomas Midgley's Freon — the compound he breathed in and blew out a candle with, the compound that sold eight million refrigerators and freed families from the terror of toxic fumes — is molecule by molecule eating the ozone layer that protects all life on Earth.

CYRUS: When do people figure this out?

JEFF: Not until nineteen seventy-four. Forty-four years after Midgley's demonstration. In nineteen eighty-five, the ozone hole over Antarctica is confirmed. Nineteen eighty-seven, the Montreal Protocol is signed, phasing out CFCs.

CYRUS: And Midgley never knew.

JEFF: Never knew. Because Thomas Midgley died in nineteen forty-four, thirty years before anyone discovered what his invention was doing. And the way he died... In nineteen forty, he contracts polio, which leaves him severely disabled. He invents an elaborate rope-and-pulley system to help lift himself out of bed. On November second, nineteen forty-four, he becomes entangled in the ropes and strangles to death. Killed by his own invention.

CYRUS: A man who invented leaded gasoline and Freon... both environmental catastrophes... dies killed by a third invention. Before learning what any of them did.

JEFF: A historian will later call Midgley "the most environmentally destructive person who ever lived"... a man who had "more impact on the atmosphere than any other single organism in Earth's history."

CYRUS: [pause] And the kicker is... he was trying to HELP. The leaded gasoline was supposed to stop engine knock. The Freon was supposed to stop families from being poisoned. He solved real problems. He saved real lives. The consequences just took decades to arrive.

JEFF: That's the thing about this whole story. Every solution creates a new problem. The pattern never breaks.

CYRUS: Which brings us to the big question. Could we go back?

JEFF: Imagine your power goes out tonight. Not for a few hours. For a week. Your food is gone in two days. The grocery store's food is gone in two days. The hospital's insulin, the blood bank's supply, the vaccines... all compromised. That's just your city. Now multiply that by every city on Earth.

We have built a world that physically cannot function at ambient temperature.

CYRUS: Seven billion people fed through the cold chain. Hundreds of millions living in climates made habitable only by air conditioning. The dependency is total. And it's irreversible.

--- SEGMENT: Close ---
